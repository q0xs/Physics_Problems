# Task 11.01 – De Broglie Wavelength

## Problem Statement

Find the de Broglie wavelength of a $150\text{ eV}$ electron and a $50\text{ g}$ golf ball moving at $60\text{ m/s}$. Explain why macroscopic quantum effects are not noticeable.

## Theory

Matter wavelength is Planck's constant divided by momentum. For a nonrelativistic electron, momentum comes from kinetic energy.

$$
\lambda=\frac{h}{p}
$$

$$
p=\sqrt{2mK}
$$

## Step-by-Step Solution

$$
K=150(1.602\times10^{-19})=2.403\times10^{-17}\text{ J}
$$

$$
p_e=\sqrt{2(9.109\times10^{-31})(2.403\times10^{-17})}=6.62\times10^{-24}\text{ kg m/s}
$$

$$
\lambda_e=1.00\times10^{-10}\text{ m}
$$

$$
p_{ball}=(0.050)(60)=3.0\text{ kg m/s}
$$

$$
\lambda_{ball}=\frac{6.626\times10^{-34}}{3.0}=2.21\times10^{-34}\text{ m}
$$

## Final Result

$$
\lambda_e=1.00\times10^{-10}\text{ m}
$$

$$
\lambda_{ball}=2.21\times10^{-34}\text{ m}
$$

## Interpretation

The electron wavelength is atomic in scale, while the golf-ball wavelength is far too small to observe.
