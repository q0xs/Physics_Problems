# Task 05 – Runner Jumping onto a Cart

## Problem Statement

A runner of mass $70\ \mathrm{kg}$ moves at $3\ \mathrm{m/s}$ and jumps onto a stationary cart of mass $140\ \mathrm{kg}$.

Determine:

1. the final speed of the cart and runner together,
2. whether kinetic energy is conserved.

## Theory

When two bodies stick together, the collision is perfectly inelastic.

### Momentum conservation

In the absence of external horizontal forces,

$$
m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2 = (m_1 + m_2) v_f
$$

### Kinetic energy

Kinetic energy is generally not conserved in an inelastic collision. To verify this, compare:

$$
K_i = \frac{1}{2}m_1 v_1^2 + \frac{1}{2}m_2 v_2^2
$$

and

$$
K_f = \frac{1}{2}(m_1 + m_2) v_f^2
$$

## Step-by-Step Solution

### 1. Final speed

The runner has

$$
m_1 = 70\ \mathrm{kg}, \qquad v_1 = 3\ \mathrm{m/s}
$$

The cart has

$$
m_2 = 140\ \mathrm{kg}, \qquad v_2 = 0
$$

Using momentum conservation,

$$
m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2 = (m_1 + m_2) v_f
$$

Since the cart is initially at rest,

$$
70 \cdot 3 = (70 + 140) v_f
$$

$$
210 = 210 v_f
$$

Thus,

$$
v_f = 1.0\ \mathrm{m/s}
$$

### 2. Initial kinetic energy

Initially only the runner has kinetic energy:

$$
K_i = \frac{1}{2} \cdot 70 \cdot 3^2
$$

$$
K_i = 35 \cdot 9 = 315\ \mathrm{J}
$$

### 3. Final kinetic energy

After the collision, the total mass is

$$
m_1 + m_2 = 210\ \mathrm{kg}
$$

and the common speed is $1.0\ \mathrm{m/s}$. Therefore,

$$
K_f = \frac{1}{2} \cdot 210 \cdot 1^2 = 105\ \mathrm{J}
$$

### 4. Compare energies

The change in kinetic energy is

$$
\Delta K = K_f - K_i = 105 - 315 = -210\ \mathrm{J}
$$

Thus, $210\ \mathrm{J}$ of kinetic energy is lost.

## Final Result

The final speed of the cart and runner is

$$
v_f = 1.0\ \mathrm{m/s}
$$

Kinetic energy is **not** conserved.

Initial and final kinetic energies are

$$
K_i = 315\ \mathrm{J}, \qquad K_f = 105\ \mathrm{J}
$$

## Interpretation

Momentum is conserved because there is no external horizontal impulse of significance during the jump. Kinetic energy decreases because this is a perfectly inelastic collision. The lost kinetic energy is converted into internal energy, deformation, vibrations, and heat.
